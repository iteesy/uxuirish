Summary

In 2020, VMworld had to quickly turn around to hold its first ever 48-hour virtual event rather than in person. The following year, the events team wanted to fill the void for the lack of networking and communication by partnering with our team to rebuild our existing live audio communication app and cater it to their 2021 theme, Imagine That.

Background

The main event
VMworld is our main annual global technology conference with over 20,000+ attendees that for a portion of IT folks, highlight anticipation, and even consider it the highlight of their career in retrospect. Unfortunately, I never got an opportunity to witness it in person at the SF Convention Center, but I couldn’t help but get severe FOMO from participants interviewed for our research.

Because the first-ever virtual event in 2020 had a severe lack of legendary presence, besides John Legend as guest star, the anticipation wasn’t as high — attendees would open several sessions, watch the keynote speakers, and the grand performance, and then call it a day. There was no feeling of bumping into someone in front of the booths or grabbing a drink with your customers.

Unbeknownst at the same time, our team was struggling to gain traction for our social audio app and reaching out to prospective users and event coordinators to get a glimpse of VMware’s newest innovation. Unfortunately, other conferences were thoroughly planned in advance already, and that a sudden new tool was too immature and quite the gamble at the time.

But who knew the biggest event of the company would be so willing to be our first partner to fill the void in both our ends.

🚩 Problem

How can we bring a communal networking environment to a virtual event?

High-level Goals

  1. Educate colleagues as to why some words in their content may be non-inclusive.
  2. Review content for non-inclusive terms validated by the etymology team.
  3. Suggest alternative words and allow other suggestions.

Disclaimer

In retrospect, there is a boom in live audio communication apps in the market that more or less create similar goals nowadays.
I know. I can vindicate that I wasn’t the original designer and part of the first project team and that the cards later shuffled to my hands, but I can vouch that the creation of this application came before Clubhouse came into the picture.

The pervasiveness of social audio apps in 2021 later did cause a bigger challenge for us in the future and a greater discourse to navigate around.

Retro, Research, & Readiness

I can't stress the amount of workshops and research initiatives were put into the early stages of this app and how important they were. The number of Miro boards far exceeds the number of Figma files.

We had researchers discovering possible pathways the application can take with potential users and our team held retros to uncover why past versions were of low traction. The prioritization matrices were getting revisited again and again.

On my end, I got to facilitate these design thinking workshops so all cross-functional roles can align with a vision.

Designs

The final design went through several iterations and levels of approvals with our teams. I worked closely with the designers of the agency working with the VMworld team to adhere to Brand guidelines with a VMworld Design System, and co-create a cohesive experience throughout the event.

Each design was tested with confirmed attendees of the event.

Outcome

Big Wins & Learnings
From my personal and career growth, one of the gradual milestones throughout the product’s development was being able to facilitate design thinking workshops to map our strategic focuses. At the time, the team was in a twilight zone era of working with each other emotionally from past sprint issues, so it was a rewarding experience to see the morale go up again based on how you laid out the activities. I’d like to think of it as being the teacher who does the assigned seating and puts you with your crush.

I used to blindly believe that as a UX designer, the people I work with don’t matter as much as how interesting the project is to me. I was terribly wrong -- in fact, I realized that the people I work with all along with play a huge role in my career satisfaction and motivation. In retrospect, this even applies to my retail experience when I closed night shifts at Daiso.

[Cue: “Maybe the real UX is the friends we made along the way.”]

Now on the technical side of this project, there’s much to unpack that we as a team failed and learned together: the nature of the beast that is corporate events. Last-minute requirements and decisions were being made that many of our typical review and approval steps had to be skipped or skimmed. For a decade-run event, it wasn’t what we expected but the paths were already forged with no way going back.

Because this tool was also part of the many integrations of the event, other vendors faced the same challenges, and working with the main event platform had its consequences with testing the last remaining weeks of the event, leading to many more edge cases to cover from every role.

How we handled this? In the grand scheme by how in-demand this event is, there’s no tippy-toeing around other than pushing through every hour that counted in a day. I can vouch that we did the best we can at every step (to the point where I can sleep without thinking of what-ifs) -- tried to push for planning more than a quarter in advance, spoke to every LT in relation, but if nothing budges with so many cooks in the kitchen, then we all fall together.

Aftermath

In summary,

  • Discoverability issues were inevitable since this was a 2 day event crammed with many opportunities that can overwhelm an attendee.
  • Getting free stuff at any event will always trump any activity with no free stuff.
  • In-app reactions are a must because emojis are crucial part of netspeak.
  • Always, always listen to the research results and advocate for more time in the discovery phase.

Currently the app is being refactored to meet internal needs.
