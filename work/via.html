Via Link Shortener

Project Details

Type: Link management platform, QR generator, Link shortener
Company: VMware
Platform: Web app, Mobile
Duration: Feb 2020 - Aug 2020, Feb 2021
Team: 3 Engineers, 1 QA, 2 Designers, 1 Project Manager, 1 Product Manager
Tools: Figma, Dovetail, Excel / Notion, Jira

Summary - From Side Project to Spotlight

Similar to the culture at Google, R&D colleagues at VMware love creating their own pet projects in hope that can benefit the rest of the company. Usually these pet projects leave empty-handed due to maintenance or low resources, so this was the first time an idea from my team came to light in a big scale.
‍
With no official link shortener tool at VMware, our team created one ourselves but just a little smarter — inspired by our very own pain point from helping with an event which resonated with many others in our research. We successfully pitched this to the LT of VMware IT as a viable tool to produce for the company then this eventually became the most popular internal tool through word of mouth, saving character space across BUs.

Background

Origin Story
To kick off the new (and later known as "cursed") year 2020, VMware’s Genius-like hub for IT support, Oasis, planned a grand launch party for a new building relocation. They requested help from us to get their event feeling upscale and promoted.

My team was tasked to provide UI for our temi robots to market the event around campus and configure it to ask attendees to submit feedback during the refreshments break. I designed informational screens for our temis and our developers built them out while making sure the robots didn’t walk off to our turtle ponds.
‍
It was primarily a small innocent task and actually, my first task at the company! At the time, I was only two weeks in, so you can imagine the mix of anxiety and excitement stirring my insides as much I wanted to exceed expectations. I got to work with a robot, everyone!

As I was taking baby steps to learn the don’t-dos at a big company, I was “highly discouraged” to use Bitly to shorten the long links for the event prize and feedback survey. We settled to display a QR code to scan, but then that opened a can of worms for our team to spend more time on edge cases for a QR code.

If the first 200 people who scan the QR code wins a free t-shirt, what will happen if you’re the 201+ person? How many people scanned it? Which devices are they using, Android or iOS? How will we get all the stats on our QR code if we need to prove how successful the event marketing was?

We noticed a growing pain point that colleagues dealing with QR codes and short URLs were using a plethora of third-party apps, but never streamlining the data and edge cases behind them. So at last, that’s where our developers slid the note under the door to our directors to propose an official internal tool for this, and we went from there.

Temi robots in action for the event.

🚩 Problem

How can we optimize links for colleagues to better engage with their content?

Early Challenges

No idea is original, ever
‍Working in big tech means there are thousands of bright needles in the haystacks. It’s a common occurrence that multiple people have the same idea or the same product name and that R&D folks tend to have an “I’ll do it myself” attitude which I respect, meaning some folks already built a link shortener.

“Go” in this case, was their simple hole-in-wall, home-brewed project that just lived on for decades at the company. Its legacy spread through word of mouth in the R&D community for generations with a devout user base of principal and senior engineers.

Before we went off creating this solution, there were multiple to-dos to take care of first. Investigating through the depths of Slack to contact the current Go owners - how can we transition the current Go users to embrace our new link shortener? And more importantly, user and problem space research to kick us off at the right push.

The original link shortener at VMware created in the 2000s and left untouched.

Research & Validation

Discovery & Ideation
‍Our initial research with prospective users proved this solution as significantly viable for not only non-technical roles such as marketing, events, and talent acquisition but R&D engineers as well. Engineer users of Go, the original lowkey link shortener, evangelize tidy code for sparking joy, so shortening links for code reviews, Confluence and Bugzilla pages, and front-end files was second nature.

Although open to this solution, the grim concern from senior engineers though was the future for their Go links that were already scattered across their code files. There was no way they would rectify each one or else digital pitchforks might arise. Terminating Go entirely might have even resulted in the downfall of VMware products and the company - but that’s just an exaggeration. We knew there had to be a seamless transition in the backend.

On the other hand, QR codes (pre-covid!) were generated at an abundance for event coordinators. One wished a smart QR solution existed for a conference session when they accidentally generated the wrong link for the already largely printed QR and could not edit the direct link. This later became helpful when VMware launched it’s back-to-office posters around campus.

Competitive Analysis
We researched and tested out various URL shorteners on the market along with QR code generators such as Bitly, TinyURL, and QRCode Monkey.

Functional Requirements

Once we gathered user requirements by researching the problem scope that consisted of interviewing possible users at a global scale and how this tool may or may not be of benefit to their workflow, findings were communicated with our engineers and PM to prioritize into feasible requirements for the first launch.

  1. Generate a short link and QR code for long URLs
  2. Manage link settings
  3. Create unique restrictions
  4. Summarize analytics
  5. Shared link management [1.2]
  6. Transfer of Ownership [1.2]

Research

Validating the problem
Was this solution even needed? We were weary to be optimistic about the deliverable. Our engineers were skeptical of the need for this solution, which was where I conducted interviews with VMware content creators and writers to access their processes and pain points.

When the initiative kick-started, I found that VMware writers had to do damage control with past and current document content. In the initial user research, we found colleagues were doing ctrl/cmd+F for each non-inclusive word on the terminology list by muscle memory! At the same time, they had the list open on another tab or monitor to check the recommended alternatives. Now, repeat this with hundreds of documents going from one page to over a hundred then times that with thousands of colleagues.

On a higher level issue, Acrolinx was the official writing assistant tool that was on the brink to cease as the multi-million dollar licensing and need for Acrolinx engineers proved too costly to maintain.

Early Designs

Each iteration from sketch, wireframe, and high fidelity were testing amongst users. Since this application was going from 0-to-1, there's a lot of experiments to pick and pull from to create the best user-friendly layout.

From early designs tested, the following had to be redesigned based on team and user feedback:

  1. Customize URL: Not only was the placeholder text too developer-friendy, the option to create a custom URL at the first step was entirely removed. Users found having two input fields, one for entering the URL and another for entering an optional custom URL name, initially confusing for first time and fast usage. This was moved later in the flow to prioritize fast short link generation.
  2. Link Settings: Link settings were moved later in the flow as a feedback after creating a short link because more link setting options were added and users felt that setting up these specific parameters weren't an immediate need to set right after creating a link.
  3. QR Code Generator: The placement of the user journey was moved for the same reason as Link Settings, but the sliding interaction itself, thought fun and friendly, proved not fitting for this experience because of the Low to High Quality scale and restriction feature of "Limit number of scans" confusing for users on first impression. This was changed to restrictions being placed in its own Restrictions tab and the slider being to fixed increments of sizes (px). For our users dealing with print, this later added an option to download an appropriate file format of their choice.
  4. Quick Actions: Initially, quick actions were placed above to set if the link will be kept internally along with a tooltip explanation, a copy link, and create QR action. Internal was moved to a Restrictions tab that was created later on, a double copy link button was redundant, and creating a QR code was place more prominently with Link Settings and Restrictions so that users know that's visibily a main feature to use.
  5. Link Logs: When a user returns to Via after creating short links, a log of links display as tiles. This was later reorganized to make it seamless responsively on smaller resolutions and mobile.

From user testing sessions for the final iterations, we recorded our quantitative results by number of errors and highlights for each task exploring a specific feature that was iterated until it led to the final design below.

Outcome

The aftermath
Marketing our release was limited to one-time virtual events -- IT trainings, intranet articles, and Slack announcements with a fraction of expected viewers as we were hit with a companywide burnout at the time. As the initial hit waned, Via experienced an exponential growth, becoming our top used tool. Our KPIs were greatly met via Adoption, Stickiness, and Growth with the north star metric being the number of short links created .

To this today, every once in a while we’d receive fan-mail or list of suggestions from Via fans that we consider in our release plannings.

How It Evolved!

This case study walks through the zero-to-one journey to the first version so there was always room for improvement. For example, I partnered up with VMware's accessibility team to remediate a11y issues that can burden the experience for low-sighted users or users relying on assisted technology.

Eventually, because I was also doubling down time with my main project, SmartSpaces, this tool was later offboarded to another designer on my team. From user feedback, prioritization for the next release involved shared link management with other colleagues and transferring links to your manager if an employee were to leave the company.

The plot twist is that Via eventually ricocheted back to me to work on its Developer Experience feature!
